# GranWatch — App Store Screenshot Shot List

Screenshots are your single highest-converting App Store asset. Most users decide to download (or not) before they read a single word of your description. This shot list specifies exactly which screens to capture, in what order, in what state, and what caption to overlay.

---

## Required Sizes

| Platform | Device | Dimensions | Orientation | Min / Max |
|----------|--------|------------|-------------|-----------|
| Apple App Store | iPhone 6.7" (iPhone 16 Pro Max) | **1290 × 2796 px** | Portrait | 3 minimum, 10 maximum |
| Apple App Store | iPhone 5.5" (iPhone 8 Plus) | **1242 × 2208 px** | Portrait | 3 minimum, 10 maximum |
| Google Play | Any Android device | **16:9 ratio** (e.g. 1920 × 1080 px) | Portrait or landscape accepted; portrait preferred | 2 minimum, 8 maximum |

Submit the same 8 shots for all three size requirements. Resize/crop as needed per platform. Consistent content across sizes.

---

## Shot Order and Specifications

### Shot 1 — Dashboard (HOME SCREEN)

**Screen in the app:** Home / Dashboard — the main feed showing all gran profiles

**UI state to show:**
- At least 3 gran profiles listed (e.g. "Gran", "Ouma", "Nana" — use realistic names)
- Profile 1: green status ring (visited recently)
- Profile 2: yellow status ring (getting close to threshold)
- Profile 3: orange status ring (overdue)
- Each card shows: profile photo (use realistic placeholder face photos), gran's name, days since last visit count, status colour
- At least one notification badge or "nudge" indicator visible if available
- The UI should look busy and alive — a dashboard with one gran looks like a demo; three grans looks like a real family app

**Caption overlay text:**
> "At a glance — always know how Gran is doing"

**Caption placement:** Top third of the screenshot, above the content. Large bold sans-serif. White text on a soft dark scrim or coloured background band.

**Why this is Shot 1:** First impression. The status ring dashboard is the core value proposition made visual. Someone scrolling the App Store immediately sees "this shows me if Gran is okay." That is the hook.

---

### Shot 2 — Gran Profile (RING DETAIL)

**Screen in the app:** Individual gran profile page / elder detail screen

**UI state to show:**
- One gran's full profile — the large circular status ring should be green and full (recently visited, all good)
- Gran's name and profile photo prominently displayed inside or adjacent to the ring
- Days since last visit counter visible (e.g. "Last visited 2 days ago" or "2 days")
- The "Log a Visit" button visible below the ring
- Family members section showing at least 2–3 avatars of family members who belong to this gran's group

**Caption overlay text:**
> "One tap tells you everything"

**Caption placement:** Top third, above the ring. Alternatively centred below the ring if the ring is in the top half.

**Why Shot 2:** After seeing the dashboard, the user wants to know what tapping a gran card shows them. This shot answers that: a big, clear, emotionally readable ring with the full profile context.

---

### Shot 3 — Log a Visit (VISIT MODAL)

**Screen in the app:** Log Visit modal / sheet (the overlay that appears when you tap "Log a Visit")

**UI state to show:**
- The visit logging modal is open and partially filled in
- Date field: today's date pre-filled
- Notes field: show a realistic note like "Had tea with Gran, she seemed happy and chatty. Showed her the garden." (This makes the feature tangible — users instantly understand what this is for)
- A "Save Visit" or "Log Visit" button clearly visible
- The gran's name visible at the top of the modal
- Optional: a small emoji or mood selector if it exists in the UI

**Caption overlay text:**
> "Log a visit in seconds"

**Caption placement:** Top of the screenshot above the modal, or on a colour band at the top.

**Why Shot 3:** Addresses the "how does this work in practice?" question. The logging action is the primary user behaviour — showing it in action reassures users it's simple and quick. The example note text does the job of explaining the feature without any extra copy.

---

### Shot 4 — History Tab (MERGED TIMELINE)

**Screen in the app:** History / Activity tab — the merged timeline of visits and gifts

**UI state to show:**
- A scrollable timeline of events — at least 5–6 entries visible
- Mix of visit entries ("Visit logged by Sarah, 3 days ago") and gift entries ("Flowers sent by James, 1 week ago")
- Each entry shows: person who did the action, action type, date, and any note snippet
- Different family member avatars visible on different entries — this communicates that multiple people are contributing
- At least one gift/flower send entry to show the gifting feature exists alongside visits
- Timeline should look active — a family using the app regularly, not a sparse demo

**Caption overlay text:**
> "Every family moment, tracked together"

**Caption placement:** Top of screenshot.

**Why Shot 4:** Shows the social/collaborative nature of the app. It's not just one person tracking — it's the whole family contributing. This is a key differentiator vs. a single-user reminder app.

### Shot 5 — Care Schedule (GRAN+ FEATURE)

**Screen in the app:** Care Schedule tab — medications and doctor appointments (Gran+ paid feature)

**UI state to show:**
- At least 2 medication entries (e.g. "Blood pressure tablet — 8am daily", "Vitamin D — with breakfast")
- At least 1 upcoming appointment (e.g. "Dr Patel — GP — Thursday 14:00")
- Gran+ badge or indicator visible — this shot doubles as a paywall advertisement
- The schedule should look practical and populated, not empty
- A clear add button (+ or "Add medication") visible — shows the feature is user-editable

**Caption overlay text:**
> "Care schedule — medication reminders and appointments"

**Caption placement:** Top of screenshot.

**Why Shot 5:** Demonstrates the Gran+ value proposition. Users who need this feature will upgrade. Even users on the free tier see this and understand where the app is going. It also positions GranWatch as a genuine care coordination tool, not just a visit tracker.

### Shot 6 — Send Flowers / Gifting (GIFTING BUTTONS)

**Screen in the app:** Gifting screen or the section of the gran profile that shows "Send Gran Love" / gifting options

**UI state to show:**
- The gifting buttons clearly visible: Flowers, Chocolates, Grocery delivery (or whichever gift types are implemented)
- Each button has an icon and label
- The gran's name visible at the top or in the section header (e.g. "Send Gran a gift")
- Optionally: a "recently sent" indicator or history snippet showing someone already sent flowers this week

**Caption overlay text:**
> "Send Gran love, from anywhere in the world"

**Caption placement:** Top of screenshot.

**Why Shot 6:** This is an emotional shot. "From anywhere in the world" speaks directly to the diaspora user — the SA expat in Sydney whose mum is in Cape Town. The gifting feature is a paid/premium unlock and a genuine differentiator. This shot also drives the partnership revenue story if gifting has a commercial component.

### Shot 7 — Family Members (MEMBERS LIST)

**Screen in the app:** Family Members / People tab — the list of family members in a gran's group

**UI state to show:**
- At least 4–5 family members listed with avatars and names (use realistic names: Sarah, James, Thabo, Emma, Liesl)
- Each member shows their role or last activity (e.g. "Last visit: 3 days ago" or "Joined 2 weeks ago")
- A clear "Invite Family Member" button visible
- Mix of profile photos and initials avatars — realistic for a real family where some members have profile photos and some don't

**Caption overlay text:**
> "The whole family, finally in one place"

**Caption placement:** Top of screenshot.

**Why Shot 7:** Communicates the network effect and the family-as-unit design. A user considering the app is already imagining which family members they'd invite. This screenshot triggers that mental exercise explicitly.

---

### Shot 8 — Notifications (NOTIFICATION LIST)

**Screen in the app:** Notifications / Alerts tab

**UI state to show:**
- At least 4–5 notification entries visible
- Mix of types: "Gran hasn't been visited in 7 days — nudge the family", "James logged a visit with Ouma", "Sarah sent flowers to Gran", "Nana's appointment tomorrow: Dr Patel 14:00"
- Some notifications read, some unread — realistic inbox state
- The GranWatch logo or app icon visible in the notification UI if it appears there

**Caption overlay text:**
> "Never miss a nudge — we've got you"

**Caption placement:** Top of screenshot.

**Why Shot 8:** Closes the loop on the core value prop. The notifications screenshot shows that GranWatch is proactive — it doesn't wait for you to remember, it tells you when action is needed. "We've got you" is reassuring and warm, in line with the brand voice.

## Google Play Screenshot Note

For Google Play, the same 8 shots work. Google Play accepts portrait screenshots at any resolution with a 16:9 aspect ratio. If you're producing separate Google Play assets:

- Crop the iPhone 6.7" screenshots to 9:16 portrait (they're already portrait — just ensure the caption overlay fits within the crop zone)
- Alternatively, produce at 1080 × 1920 px for Google Play (standard Android portrait)
- Google Play also accepts landscape (16:9 horizontal) feature graphics — consider producing a 1920 × 1080 px landscape version of Shot 1 (Dashboard) as the optional Feature Graphic in the Play Store listing

## Producing the Screenshots Efficiently

### Recommended workflow

1. **Take raw screenshots on the device** (or iOS Simulator in Xcode at the correct resolution). Run the app, navigate to each screen, set up the correct UI state as described above, then screenshot. For iPhone 6.7": use an iPhone 16 Pro Max or Simulator set to that device. For 5.5": use Simulator set to iPhone 8 Plus.

2. **Add the device frame and caption overlay using a screenshot tool.** Do not deliver raw screenshots — the frame and caption text are essential for App Store conversion.

**Recommended tools:**

- **[Previewed.app](https://previewed.app)** — browser-based, drag-and-drop, supports iPhone 16 Pro Max frame, custom caption text, background gradients. Free tier available. Good for producing consistent branded screenshots fast. Best option for a first pass.
- **[AppShots.net](https://appshots.net)** — similar to Previewed, simpler interface, free.
- **[Figma](https://figma.com)** — if you have a designer, they can produce pixel-perfect screenshots using device mockup frames from the Figma community (search "iPhone 16 Pro Max mockup"). Full control over typography, colour, branding. Best final quality.
- **[DaVinci Resolve / ScreenFloat / CleanShot X]** — for annotating and exporting, if you prefer a native macOS tool.

### Caption style guide

- Font: Bold, sans-serif (SF Pro, Inter, or similar). Match the brand.
- Text colour: White (#FFFFFF) or brand colour.
- Background: Either a solid colour band at the top of the screenshot, or a soft dark gradient scrim behind the text (so caption is readable regardless of the screen content underneath).
- Keep captions short — the ones above are exactly the right length. Do not write paragraph-length captions; they will not be read.

### Order matters

Submit the shots in the order listed above (1–8). App Store and Play Store show screenshots sequentially. Shot 1 must be your strongest hook — the Dashboard with multiple gran profiles is that shot.
